Gamification & AI Daily Motivation

Exploring how AI-powered game mechanics can make daily habit-building feel rewarding rather than punishing.

My Role
UX Researcher & Designer
Timeline
8 weeks
Tools
Figma, FigJam, Notion
Type
Concept Case Study
🎮

The Problem

Most habit apps fail users within two weeks. They're either too rigid — miss a day and your streak resets — or too passive, with no loop to keep you coming back. The core question: what if a habit app knew when to push you and when to give you a break?

Research & Discovery

I analyzed 8 habit apps including Habitica, Duolingo, Finch, Fabulous, Streaks, Bereal, Forest, and Todoist, and ran 5 user interviews with people who had tried and abandoned habit apps.

  • Users wanted the app to understand them. Generic reminders felt hollow and were quickly disabled.
  • Gamification works when rewards feel earned, not automatic. Points for just opening the app felt meaningless.
  • The most motivating features were social: leaderboards, shared streaks, accountability partners.
  • AI felt exciting but threatening if it felt like surveillance. Transparency about AI reasoning was the biggest trust lever.

Design Approach

I designed around three core mechanics: an adaptive AI coach that adjusts tone and challenge based on recent behavior; a quest-based task system that frames habits as an ongoing story rather than a checkbox list; and a flexible streak recovery system that rewards consistency over perfection.

Key Design Decisions

  • Adaptive Coach: AI messages shift from encouraging to challenging based on streak data, not a fixed daily prompt.
  • Recovery Quest: Missing a day triggers a special challenge that, if completed, restores the streak. Rewards consistency over perfection.
  • A character avatar that levels up visually with long-term progress — persistent reward for sustained effort.
  • All AI suggestions are opt-in and show the reasoning behind each recommendation.

Results

Concept validation

  • All 3 prototype participants preferred the recovery quest over streak resets, calling it fair and more human.
  • The adaptive coach scored higher on trust when tone rationale was visible to the user.
  • Quest framing was the most novel feature — the one most likely to keep participants engaged past 2 weeks.

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • Gamification done poorly feels manipulative. Done well, it makes the user feel capable. The difference is whether mechanics respect user autonomy.
  • AI in UX is a positioning problem as much as a design problem. Transparency about why the AI is making a suggestion was the single biggest trust lever I found.
  • I had a critical assumption wrong. I designed the recovery quest to be hard. Most participants wanted it easy — they'd already failed and needed a win, not another obstacle.
