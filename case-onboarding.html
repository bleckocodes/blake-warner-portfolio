SaaS Onboarding Redesign

Redesigning a board management SaaS platform's onboarding experience to improve new-user activation and cut time-to-first-action.

My Role
Lead UX Designer
Timeline
12 weeks (Capstone)
Tools
Figma, FigJam, Maze
Context
Heroic Development
🧭

The Problem

Onboarding is the moment a new user decides whether to stay or leave. The existing experience dropped users into a blank dashboard with minimal guidance, leaving them unsure where to start and what to do next. Users who did not complete a core action in their first session rarely returned.

Users and Research

I conducted discovery interviews with 6 target users - administrators and team leads responsible for managing organizational meetings and documentation. I also ran a competitive analysis of onboarding patterns across Notion, Monday.com, ClickUp, Loom, and Asana.

  • Users felt overwhelmed by too many options at once - they needed a single clear first step to anchor to.
  • The existing onboarding skipped explaining the product's core value - users were not sure what success looked like.
  • Users expected progress indicators to know how close they were to finishing setup.
  • Personalization cues like role selection made users feel the product was built specifically for them.

Design Process

Discovery to Define

I mapped the current new-user journey and identified 4 critical drop-off moments. I developed user personas based on my interviews and set a primary design goal: get new users to complete their first core action within 5 minutes of signing up.

Ideation to Wireframing

I wireframed 3 competing onboarding flows focused on progressive disclosure - showing users only the options relevant to their current step. I ran a design critique with my capstone team to pressure-test each approach before committing to one direction.

Prototyping to Testing

I built a high-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma and conducted 5 moderated usability tests. Participants received a task prompt and no additional instructions. I observed where they hesitated, where they succeeded, and where they gave up.

Key Design Decisions

  • Reduced onboarding from 9 steps to 4 by deferring configuration options that could be set later in the product.
  • Added a persistent progress bar so users always knew how close they were to completing setup.
  • Introduced role selection at step 1 to personalize the rest of the flow based on the user's answer.
  • Replaced the empty dashboard with a quick-start checklist surfacing 3 recommended next actions on first login.

Results

Outcomes

  • 4 of 5 usability test participants completed onboarding without assistance, up from 2 of 5 in the original flow.
  • Average time-to-first-action dropped from 8+ minutes to under 3.5 minutes in prototype testing.
  • Final design approved by stakeholders in a single revision cycle.
  • Delivered annotated Figma handoff file and complete flow documentation to the development team.

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • Less is more in onboarding. Every step I removed improved completion rates. My instinct was to show users everything the product could do - the research told me to show them one thing at a time.
  • Testing early is humbling and necessary. My first prototype had a critical navigation assumption that every single participant got wrong. Without watching real users interact with it, I never would have caught it.
  • Onboarding is a product promise. The clarity and tone of the first-run experience sets expectations for everything that follows. Designing it well is designing trust.
