VR Skill Labs Discovery

Discovery-phase research into VR adoption in classrooms. The #1 barrier was classroom management, not content.

My Role
UX Researcher & Discovery Lead
Timeline
6 weeks
Tools
FigJam, Zoom, Notion, Google Forms
Phase
Discovery (no prototyping)
🥽

Project Overview

VR has significant promise as a training tool, but the gap between what it can theoretically do and what actually works in a classroom is large. This discovery project set out to understand needs, frustrations, and opportunities around VR adoption in educational settings — before any design decisions were made.

Discovery Goals

  • Who are the primary users of classroom VR, and what are their distinct needs?
  • What are the biggest barriers to VR adoption in K-12 and higher education?
  • What existing tools or workarounds are educators using to fill VR gaps?
  • Where is the highest-leverage opportunity for a new VR learning product?

Research Methods

Stakeholder Interviews

8 interviews — 5 with educators who had attempted to integrate VR, 3 with students who had experienced VR-based instruction. Sessions ran 30–40 minutes.

Competitive Landscape

Mapped 6 VR education products (Google Expeditions, Labster, zSpace, Engage, ClassVR, Prisms VR) across content depth, hardware requirements, setup ease, and classroom management features.

Survey

A 12-question survey validated qualitative themes across 24 educators.

Key Findings

  • Classroom management was the #1 barrier. Educators couldn't monitor what students were experiencing inside a headset. Cited in 7 of 8 interviews.
  • Hardware logistics killed momentum. Setting up, charging, sanitizing, and distributing 30 headsets was described as "a second job."
  • The best VR experiences were highly specific. Broad virtual field trips rated least useful; simulation-based skill training rated most valuable.
  • Students found VR memorable but inconsistent. Some sessions were immersive; others were plagued by motion sickness or technical failures.
  • 71% of educators who had tried VR said they would not use it again without better classroom management tools.

Opportunity Areas

  • Classroom management companion: A tablet tool letting educators see each student's headset view and send prompts without requiring headset removal.
  • Simulation content marketplace: A curated library of short (5–15 min) simulations mapped to specific learning objectives, searchable by subject, grade, and skill.

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • Discovery research is about resisting the urge to solve. I came in excited about VR and had to actively stop myself from sketching solutions before the research was done. The findings completely reframed the problem — the issue was everything surrounding VR, not VR itself.
  • The person asking for a solution is not always the primary user. School administrators buying headsets had completely different needs than the teachers using them.
  • Quantitative data validates qualitative data but doesn't replace it. The 71% stat was powerful for communication. But it came from the interviews, not the survey. The survey made it credible at scale.
