VR Skill Labs Discovery

Discovery-phase research into VR adoption in classrooms. The number one barrier was classroom management, not content.

My Role
UX Researcher and Discovery Lead
Timeline
6 weeks
Tools
FigJam, Zoom, Notion, Google Forms
Phase
Discovery (no prototyping)
🥽

Project Overview

VR has significant promise as a training tool - simulated environments let learners practice high-stakes skills without real-world consequences. But the gap between what VR can theoretically do and what actually works in a classroom is large. This discovery project set out to understand the needs, frustrations, and opportunities around VR adoption in educational settings before any design decisions were made.

Discovery Goals

  • Who are the primary users of classroom VR, and what are their distinct roles and needs?
  • What are the biggest barriers to VR adoption in K-12 and higher education?
  • What existing tools or workarounds are educators using to fill the gaps?
  • Where is the highest-leverage opportunity for a new VR learning product?

Research Methods

Stakeholder Interviews

8 interviews - 5 with educators who had attempted to integrate VR into their curriculum, and 3 with students who had experienced VR-based instruction. Sessions ran 30 to 40 minutes each.

Competitive Landscape

I mapped 6 existing VR education products including Google Expeditions, Labster, zSpace, Engage, ClassVR, and Prisms VR across dimensions including content depth, hardware requirements, ease of setup, and classroom management features.

Survey

A 12-question survey deployed to a panel of 24 educators to validate qualitative themes at a broader scale.

Key Findings

  • Classroom management was the number one barrier. Educators could not monitor what students were experiencing inside a headset and felt they had lost control of the room. This was cited in 7 of 8 interviews.
  • Hardware logistics killed momentum. Setting up, charging, sanitizing, and distributing 30 VR headsets before a class was described as a second job by multiple teachers.
  • The best VR experiences were highly specific. Broad virtual field trip content was rated least useful; simulation-based skill training like dissection, lab work, and machinery operation was rated most valuable.
  • Students found VR memorable but inconsistent. Some sessions were immersive and educational; others were plagued by motion sickness or technical failures.
  • Survey data: 71% of educators who had tried VR said they would not use it again without better classroom management tools.

Opportunity Areas

  • Classroom management companion: A tablet or browser tool that lets educators see what each student's headset is displaying, pause the experience, and send shared prompts without requiring headset removal.
  • Simulation content marketplace: A curated, curriculum-aligned library of short 5 to 15 minute simulation experiences mapped to specific learning objectives and searchable by subject, grade, and skill type.

Results

Discovery outcomes

  • Identified 2 high-leverage opportunity areas backed by evidence from 8 interviews and 24 survey responses.
  • 71% of surveyed educators would not reuse VR without better classroom management tools - quantifying the core problem.
  • Competitive landscape mapped across 6 products, revealing no existing solution addressed the classroom management gap.
  • Delivered a discovery report with prioritized opportunity areas, user quotes, and recommended next-phase research questions.

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • Discovery research is about resisting the urge to solve. I came into this project excited about VR as a design space and had to actively stop myself from sketching solutions before the research was done. The findings completely reframed the problem - the issue was not VR content, it was everything surrounding it.
  • The person asking for a solution is not always the primary user. School administrators buying headsets had completely different needs than the teachers using them. Designing for the buyer without understanding the teacher would have produced the wrong product entirely.
  • Quantitative data validates qualitative data but does not replace it. The 71% stat was striking and useful for stakeholder communication. But it came from the interviews, not the survey. The survey made it credible at scale - the interviews made it real.
