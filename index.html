SaaS Onboarding Redesign

Redesigning a board management SaaS platform's onboarding to improve activation and cut time-to-first-action.

My Role
Lead UX Designer
Timeline
12 weeks (Capstone)
Tools
Figma, FigJam, Maze
Context
Heroic Development
🧭

The Problem

Onboarding is the moment a new user decides whether to stay or leave. The existing experience dropped users into a blank dashboard with no guidance — leaving them unsure where to start. Users who did not complete a core action in their first session rarely returned.

Users & Research

I ran discovery interviews with 6 target users — administrators and team leads managing organizational meetings — and a competitive analysis of onboarding patterns across Notion, Monday.com, ClickUp, Loom, and Asana.

  • Users felt overwhelmed by too many options at once — they needed one clear first step.
  • The existing flow skipped explaining core value — users weren't sure what success looked like.
  • Users expected progress indicators to know how far they'd gotten through setup.
  • Personalization cues like role selection made users feel the product was built for them specifically.

Design Process

Discovery → Define

I mapped the current new-user journey and identified 4 critical drop-off moments. I developed user personas from interviews and set a primary goal: get new users to their first core action within 5 minutes of signing up.

Ideation → Wireframing

I wireframed 3 competing flows focused on progressive disclosure — showing only what was relevant to the current step. I ran a critique with my capstone team to pressure-test each approach before committing.

Prototyping → Testing

I built a high-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma and ran 5 moderated usability tests. Participants received a task prompt with no instructions — I observed where they hesitated, succeeded, and gave up.

Key Design Decisions

  • Reduced onboarding from 9 steps to 4 by deferring non-critical configuration to later sessions.
  • Added a persistent progress bar so users always knew how close they were to finishing setup.
  • Introduced role selection at step 1 to personalize the rest of the flow.
  • Replaced the empty dashboard with a quick-start checklist surfacing 3 recommended next actions.

Results

Outcomes

  • 4 of 5 usability test participants completed onboarding without assistance (up from 2 of 5 in the original flow).
  • Average time-to-first-action dropped from 8+ minutes to under 3.5 minutes in prototype testing.
  • Final design approved by stakeholders in a single revision cycle.
  • Delivered annotated Figma handoff file and complete flow documentation to development.

Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • Less is more in onboarding. Every step I removed improved completion. My instinct was to show users everything the product could do; research said show them one thing at a time.
  • Testing early is humbling and necessary. My first prototype had a navigation assumption that every single participant got wrong. Without watching real users, I never would have caught it.
  • Onboarding is a product promise. The clarity and tone of the first-run experience sets expectations for everything that follows. Designing it well is designing trust.
