Cosplay Build

Mandalorian Armor

I modeled every armor piece from scratch in Blender using reference images from The Mandalorian and my own measurements so everything fit correctly. Each piece was sliced in Cura and printed on my Neptune 3 Plus — the full build took over six months of printing, sanding, and iteration.

After printing, each piece got sanded through multiple grits, filled with spot putty to hide layer lines, then primed. I airbrushed the base coat and did all the weathering — scratches, rust streaks, and battle damage — entirely by hand using dry-brushing and washes. The chest piece alone went through four versions before I was happy with the fit. Worn at Emerald City Comic Con 2025.

Cosplay Build

Rodian Head Prosthetic

The Rodian head was modeled entirely in Blender from scratch using Star Wars reference art and screen grabs from the original films. The main challenge was making it wearable — I had to design it in segments that could be assembled around my head and actually see out of.

I printed it in flexible TPU filament so it could flex slightly for comfort and not crack under pressure. The surface texture was achieved by sculpting detail directly in Blender before printing — scales, wrinkles, and eye sockets. Painted with acrylic paints layered to get the green alien skin tone, then sealed with matte varnish. It placed as a finalist at the 2025 Boeing Museum of Flight Cosplay Contest.

Cosplay Build

Mandalorian Jetpack

The jetpack is made up of 14 separate printed pieces that bolt together with countersunk screws and align with guide pins I printed into the design. The whole thing mounts to a plate that straps to my back using a repurposed backpack harness — it has to stay secure while I'm walking around a convention floor all day.

The thruster nozzles have internal LED strips wired to a small battery pack hidden inside the main body — they glow orange to simulate the heat of the jets. For the weathering, I used a heat gun carefully over the painted surface to create real heat distortion in the plastic, then layered burnt sienna and black washes into the crevices. Completed and debuted at ECCC 2025.

Prop Build

Screen-Accurate Blasters

I modeled both blasters in Blender using reference photos, prop documentation, and measurements sourced from the prop-making community. Screen accuracy was the goal — every greeble, panel line, and detail was modeled in before printing.

Printed in PLA, assembled with wood screws and epoxy where pieces needed to be permanent. The realistic metal finish is achieved with Rub 'n Buff — a wax-based metallic compound applied by hand with a cloth. You build it up in layers, buffing between coats, until the surface reads as real gunmetal. Battle damage was added with a small wire brush and black wash settled into the scratches.

Design + Making

Sticker Design

I design stickers in Procreate on iPad using custom brushes I've built up over time. The process starts with a rough sketch, then I tighten it into clean line art, add color and shading, and export as a high-res PNG.

For cutting, I import into Silhouette Studio and trace the outline to create a cut path. The Silhouette Cameo 2 cuts the vinyl with precision — I've been experimenting with matte, gloss, and holographic vinyl depending on the design. Kiss-cut or die-cut depending on the shape. Currently working on a D&D cat class sticker series and MTG-themed accessories to sell at Cardtropolis on commander nights and through an Etsy shop under bleckocrafts.

3D / Animation

Blender Animation

A short 3D animation project built entirely in Blender. I modeled the assets from scratch, rigged them with an armature system for animation, set up a three-point lighting rig, and animated using keyframes with graph editor curve editing for smooth motion.

Rendered in Cycles for physically accurate lighting and materials. Post-processing was done inside Blender's compositor — color grading, vignette, and motion blur. This project was a deep dive into Blender's full pipeline, from polygon modeling through final render output. It gave me a much stronger foundation for designing 3D-printed props because I understand how geometry, surface normals, and scale all interact.

Progress Doc

Armor Build Progress

This comparison shows what two years of iteration looks like. On the left is the armor in 2024 — functional but rough. On the right is the ECCC 2025 version after a full repaint, new pieces, and significantly more detailed weathering.

Almost every piece was reworked. The chest plate got a complete reprint at a higher resolution and a new paint scheme. The pauldrons were redesigned to sit better. The helmet visor was replaced with a tinted acrylic cut to a tighter fit. The paint process got more sophisticated — I started using an airbrush for basecoats, which gave me much smoother gradients and more control over the weathering than I had with rattle cans the first time around.

Events

Convention Cosplay

Suiting up and actually wearing the armor at events is the final test of everything — fit, durability, comfort over a full day, and how it reads to an audience. I've competed and exhibited at Emerald City Comic Con, the Boeing Museum of Flight Cosplay Contest, and Star Wars Day events across the Pacific Northwest.

Each event reveals something to improve. A piece that looked right in the garage looks different under convention lighting. A mount that felt secure at home shifts after four hours of walking. I keep notes on everything and fold the feedback back into the next build. The community aspect is also a huge part of it — connecting with other builders and cosplayers is genuinely one of the best parts of the hobby.

3D Printing

Print to Paint Process

This is what every build looks like before the real work starts. Raw PLA off the Neptune 3 Plus, layer lines visible, support marks still on the surface. Most people don't realize how much work happens between "finished print" and "finished prop."

The post-processing pipeline: remove supports, sand through 120 → 220 → 400 grit, apply spot putty to low spots and layer lines, sand again, prime with filler primer, sand once more with 400, then paint. For organic shapes like this mask, I also use a heat gun carefully to reflow the surface slightly — it softens layer lines without distorting the geometry if you move fast. The Neptune 3 Plus has been a solid machine — large build volume, reliable bed adhesion, and consistent enough that I can plan builds around its tolerances.
